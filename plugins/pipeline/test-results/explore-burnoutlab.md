## JOB MAP -- "Recover from workplace burnout while keeping my job"

+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+
| DEFINE | LOCATE | PREPARE| CONFIRM| EXECUTE| MONITOR| MODIFY | CONCLUDE|
+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+

STEP 1: DEFINE
- What the customer determines: "Am I actually burned out, or just tired?"
- Key outcome: Minimize time to understand whether this is burnout vs. normal stress
- Current pain at this step: No accessible, validated diagnostic tool. Maslach inventory is clinical, not consumer-friendly.

STEP 2: LOCATE
- What the customer gathers/finds: Recovery resources, programs, professionals
- Key outcome: Minimize time to find a credible burnout recovery program
- Current pain at this step: Overwhelming options. Hard to distinguish evidence-based from wellness influencer advice.

STEP 3: PREPARE
- What the customer sets up: Commitment to a recovery path, schedule adjustments
- Key outcome: Minimize effort to integrate recovery into current life/work schedule
- Current pain at this step: "I barely have energy to work, let alone add a recovery program."

STEP 4: CONFIRM
- What the customer verifies: "Is this the right approach for my type of burnout?"
- Key outcome: Minimize risk of investing time/money in the wrong recovery approach
- Current pain at this step: No personalization -- most programs are one-size-fits-all.

STEP 5: EXECUTE
- What the customer does: Daily/weekly recovery actions -- exercises, boundary-setting, reflection
- Key outcome: Minimize effort to maintain recovery actions while still working full-time
- Current pain at this step: Recovery advice conflicts with workplace demands ("just say no" doesn't work in many jobs).

STEP 6: MONITOR
- What the customer tracks: Energy levels, emotional state, cynicism, effectiveness
- Key outcome: Minimize time to detect whether recovery is working or stalling
- Current pain at this step: No burnout-specific tracking tools. Generic mood trackers miss burnout dimensions.

STEP 7: MODIFY
- What the customer adjusts: Recovery plan based on what's working and what's not
- Key outcome: Minimize time to adjust recovery approach when hitting plateaus
- Current pain at this step: No feedback loop in current solutions. Books can't adapt. Therapy is slow.

STEP 8: CONCLUDE
- What the customer finalizes: New sustainable work habits, relapse prevention
- Key outcome: Minimize likelihood of burnout recurrence
- Current pain at this step: Most recovery feels fragile. No maintenance/prevention framework.

HIGHEST-OPPORTUNITY STEPS: DEFINE (no good diagnostic), EXECUTE (recovery vs. work conflict), MONITOR (no burnout-specific tracking)

COMPETITIVE RISK:
BetterUp could add a burnout-specific track, but their $300+/mo pricing and enterprise focus create a moat for the consumer market. Headspace/Calm could add burnout content, but their meditation-first model conflicts with structured recovery. The biggest risk is a well-funded startup entering the same space with similar positioning. Speed to market and brand authority matter.

**Risk #1: Knowledge workers will pay $497 for a digital burnout recovery program**

- Kill criterion: <30% completion rate even with concierge support would indicate the format is fundamentally broken.

## Non-Customer Analysis

### Tier 1: "Soon-to-be" Non-customers (Edge of Market)
- **Who they are:** People who have downloaded Headspace/Calm for burnout but use it <1x/month because it doesn't address their actual burnout.
- **Why they're leaving:** Meditation feels insufficient for their level of burnout. They need something more structured and specific.
- **Estimated size:** 5-10M users of wellness apps who searched "burnout" before downloading
- **How to capture them:** The AI burnout assessment instantly differentiates BurnoutLab from meditation apps. "You don't need to meditate more -- you need a recovery plan."

### Tier 2: "Refusing" Non-customers (Consciously Rejected)
- **Who they are:** Burned-out professionals who have considered therapy but rejected it due to cost ($150-300/session), waitlists (4-8 weeks), stigma, or "I'm not THAT bad."
- **Why they refuse:** Therapy feels like admitting serious mental illness. It's expensive. The waitlists are demoralizing when you're already exhausted. And it's not specifically about burnout.
- **Estimated size:** 10-15M in the US (estimated from burnout prevalence minus therapy utilization rates)
- **How to capture them:** Position as "professional development" not "mental health treatment." Price at $497 (vs. $2,400+/year for therapy). Instant access, no waitlists. Specifically about burnout, not general mental health.

### Tier 3: "Unexplored" Non-customers (Never Considered)
- **Who they are:** Employers/HR leaders who deal with burnout through "wellness perks" (free gym, meditation app licenses) but have never considered structured burnout recovery programs.
- **Why they've never considered:** "Burnout recovery program" is not a recognized category in corporate wellness. HR buys wellness apps, EAP programs, or coaching platforms -- not burnout-specific solutions.
- **Estimated size:** 200,000+ companies spending $50B+/yr on corporate wellness
- **How to capture them:** Create the category. Frame as "burnout-driven attrition prevention" with ROI data. "Every burned-out employee who quits costs you $50K-100K. BurnoutLab costs $2,000 per team."

### Total Addressable Non-customer Opportunity
- Current market size (burnout-specific products): ~$500M
- Tier 1 opportunity: $2-5B (wellness app users seeking more)
- Tier 2 opportunity: $5-10B (therapy-averse burnout sufferers)
- Tier 3 opportunity: $10-50B (corporate wellness market)
- **Total potential market expansion: 30-100x current market for burnout-specific solutions**

# SKILL FILE ISSUES AND OBSERVATIONS

## Value Architect (SKILL.md + references/)

**Strengths:**
- Extremely thorough reference material. The VPC, JTBD, and AI research references are comprehensive and well-structured.
- Output template is detailed with clear scoring systems (Opportunity Score, Match Strength, Weighted Fit Score).
- The validation hierarchy (Level 0-5) is an excellent framework for tagging evidence quality.

**Issues Found:**
1. **No explicit guidance on segment prioritization when multiple segments exist.** The template shows "complete one per segment" but doesn't guide which segment to analyze first or how to choose. The Model Maker references a Segment Attractiveness Matrix -- the Value Architect should reference this or have its own.
2. **The output template is very long (11 sections).** For a pre-launch analysis with no real customer data, some sections (e.g., Simulated Interview Findings) add bulk without proportional value. Consider marking more sections as [OPTIONAL] for early-stage analyses.
3. **Fit Score formula could be clearer.** The formula `SUM(Opp Score x Match Strength) / SUM(Opp Scores) x 20` is correct but the "x 20" multiplier feels arbitrary and isn't explained. Why 20? It scales to 100 only if max Match Strength is 5 (5 x 20 = 100). This should be stated explicitly.
4. **Missing: How to handle when there are no high-opportunity elements.** The decision tree assumes there will be elements scoring 8+. What if none score that high? The template has a gap here.

## Model Maker (SKILL.md + references/)

**Strengths:**
- Business Model Canvas reference is excellent -- the 9 blocks are well-explained with scoring systems and real examples.
- Blue Ocean Strategy reference is comprehensive with real ERRC examples (Cirque du Soleil, Yellow Tail, Southwest, etc.).
- The output template is well-structured and forces rigor (quality checklist, financial sketch, non-customer analysis).
- Pattern classification (Freemium, Platform, Subscription, etc.) with viability checklists is very useful.

**Issues Found:**
1. **Strategy Canvas requires competitive data that may not be available at pre-launch.** The SKILL.md says "research competitor business models via web research" but doesn't provide guidance on what to do when competitors are in different categories (comparing BetterUp coaching to Headspace meditation to Udemy courses is like comparing apples to oranges on certain factors). Guidance on cross-category competitive scoring would help.
2. **Financial Sketch section asks for benchmarks that are hard to obtain for novel categories.** BurnoutLab is creating a new category -- there IS no "industry average" for AI-powered burnout recovery. The template should acknowledge this and provide guidance on selecting comparable industries.
3. **The ERRC Grid validation check requires "at least 2 factors eliminated, 2 reduced, 2 raised, 1 created."** This is good discipline but can feel forced when the natural analysis doesn't yield that many factors. The minimum should be explained as aspirational, with the caveat that fewer items with stronger rationale is better than padding.
4. **Missing: The connection between Value Architect handoff and Model Maker input is not explicitly structured.** The SKILL.md says "Receive validated value propositions from the Value Architect" but doesn't specify what format or fields to expect. A handoff schema would help.

## Mad Scientist (SKILL.md + references/)

**Strengths:**
- The experiment library (44 experiments in Strategyzer framework) is extremely useful and well-organized by evidence strength.
- ICE and RICE scoring frameworks are well-documented with calibration rules to prevent score inflation.
- Experiment card format is rigorous -- requires falsification criteria, time boxes, sample sizes, and pass/fail thresholds.
- The testing sequence (Desk Research -> Exploration -> Smoke Test -> Concierge -> MVP -> Scale) is clear and well-reasoned.
- Pivot/Persevere framework with quantitative triggers is actionable.

**Issues Found:**
1. **The SKILL.md says to "extract top assumptions from experts 1-2" but doesn't specify a maximum number.** Without a cap, you end up with 15+ assumptions and 10+ experiment cards, which is overwhelming for a pre-launch startup. Suggest adding: "Extract 8-12 assumptions. If more exist, force-rank and carry forward only the top 12."
2. **ICE and RICE scoring inconsistency.** The experiment-prioritization.md says ICE = I x C x E (multiplicative, range 1-1000), but the output template shows ICE scores like 504, 378, etc. The scoring examples in the two files use different scales (the reference uses 1-10, consistent). This is fine but should be stated once clearly.
3. **Missing: Guidance on how to handle experiments that test MULTIPLE assumptions simultaneously.** Experiment Card #2 tests both D-02 and D-04 -- this is practical but the template doesn't explain how to handle multi-assumption experiments in the inventory tracking.
4. **The output template's Evidence Dashboard section uses a visual bar `[====------]` which is nice but assumes a 10-character width.** Minor, but the scale (0/5 to 5/5) doesn't naturally map to a 10-character bar. Consider aligning the visual to the actual scale or explaining the mapping.
5. **Sprint duration is hardcoded at "2 weeks."** For concierge MVPs (which can take 8-12 weeks for a 90-day program), the sprint model breaks. The template should acknowledge that some experiments span multiple sprints and explain how to handle this in the roadmap.

## Cross-Expert Issues

1. **Handoff format is not standardized.** The Value Architect outputs a "Handoff to Next Phase" section, the Model Maker outputs a "Handoff to Mad Scientist" section. These sections contain similar but not identical fields. A shared handoff schema would ensure nothing is lost between experts.
2. **Assumption IDs are not consistent across experts.** The Value Architect uses H1-H5, the Model Maker uses numbered assumptions 1-8, and the Mad Scientist uses D-01/V-01/F-01. The Mad Scientist's convention is the best (category prefix + number). All experts should adopt this.
3. **No explicit "input spec" for any expert.** Each SKILL.md describes what the expert does and how, but none specify "I expect to receive X from the user or from the previous expert." This makes orchestration harder.
4. **The Value Architect and Model Maker both produce competitive analysis, but from different frameworks (JTBD competitive set vs. Blue Ocean Strategy Canvas).** There is some redundancy. Consider having the Value Architect focus on JTBD forces and the Model Maker focus on Strategy Canvas factors, with a clear delineation.
