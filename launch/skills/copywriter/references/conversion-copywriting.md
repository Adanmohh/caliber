# Conversion Copywriting: The Complete Copyhackers Methodology

A deep-dive reference for AI agents writing conversion copy. Based on Joanna
Wiebe's Copyhackers methodology -- the originator of the term "conversion
copywriting." This file covers VOC mining, message mining, frameworks, headline
formulas, CTA principles, email copy, landing page structure, the Seven Sweeps,
A/B testing, failure modes, and AI-native application.

---

## 1. What Conversion Copywriting Is (And Is Not)

Conversion copywriting is data-driven copy that moves the reader to "yes." The
term was coined by Joanna Wiebe, founder of Copyhackers, in 2011. Unlike brand
copywriting or content writing, conversion copywriting relies on voice-of-customer
data, proven frameworks, formulas, and persuasion techniques to produce
measurable results.

### What It Is

- Copy informed by customer research, not writer intuition
- Copy structured by proven frameworks (PAS, AIDA, 1-2-3-4)
- Copy tested through A/B experiments and iterated on data
- Copy measured by conversion rate, not creative awards

### What It Is Not

- Clever wordplay that entertains but does not convert
- Brand manifestos that sound beautiful but ask for nothing
- Content marketing (which educates; conversion copy sells)
- Copywriting based on assumptions about what customers want

### The Core Process

```
1. Research & Discovery (VOC mining, competitor analysis, audience interviews)
   |  This is the BIGGEST part of the work.
   |  "If research is not the biggest part, 99% of the time
   |   you're doing it wrong." -- Joanna Wiebe
   v
2. Strategy Synthesis (message hierarchy, awareness mapping, Rule of One)
   v
3. Writing (frameworks, formulas, VOC language insertion)
   v
4. Wireframing (copy-first layout for designers)
   v
5. Editing (the Seven Sweeps)
   v
6. Testing (A/B tests, iteration, measurement)
```

## 2. Voice of Customer (VOC) Mining

VOC mining is the foundation. Without it, you are guessing. With it, you are
using proven language that resonates because it came from the audience itself.

### Philosophy

"Do not write great copy. Steal it." Your customers are better at recognizing
and explaining value because they actually use the product and spend money on it.
The best copy comes from their mouths, not from a copywriter's imagination.

### Where to Mine VOC Data

```
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
| SOURCE                  | WHAT TO EXTRACT                             |
|-------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Amazon reviews          | Pain language (1-2 star), dream state        |
| (competing products)    | (5 star), nuanced (3-4 star)                |
|-------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Reddit / Forums         | Unfiltered problem descriptions, solutions   |
|                         | tried, objections, "what I wish existed"     |
|-------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| G2 / Capterra /         | "What do you like best?", "What do you       |
| TrustRadius             | dislike?", competitor comparisons             |
|-------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Google Reviews / Yelp   | Emotional language, specific praise/pain     |
|-------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Support tickets         | Recurring questions, confusion points,       |
|                         | praise emails, feature request language       |
|-------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Survey responses        | Open-ended: "What was going on that brought  |
| (open-ended questions)  | you to us?", "What almost stopped you?"      |
|-------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Sales call transcripts  | Objections verbatim, decision language,       |
|                         | "that's exactly what I need" moments         |
|-------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Social media comments   | Emotional reactions, public questions,        |
|                         | competitor praise/criticism                  |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

### The Before State / Dream State Framework

All VOC data sorts into two primary buckets:

**Before State** (the world the customer wants to escape):
- Pain points: what hurts, what frustrates, what is broken
- Failed solutions: what they tried that did not work
- Emotional state: stressed, overwhelmed, embarrassed, stuck
- Consequences: what the problem costs (money, time, reputation)
- Fears: what happens if nothing changes

**Dream State** (the world the customer wants to enter):
- Desired outcomes: what they want to accomplish
- Emotional state: relieved, confident, in control, proud
- Identity: who they want to become
- Specific results: numbers, timelines, tangible changes
- Freedom from: what they want to stop doing or worrying about

### The VOC Mining Process (Step by Step)

```
Step 1: GATHER raw data
  - Go to each source listed above
  - Copy exact phrases into a spreadsheet (Column A: source, B: quote)
  - Do NOT paraphrase. Capture their exact words.
  - Aim for 100+ quotes minimum for a major project
  - 50+ minimum for smaller projects

Step 2: HIGHLIGHT key phrases
  - Read through all quotes
  - Highlight vivid, emotional, or unusually specific phrases
  - Mark language that appears repeatedly across sources
  - Note metaphors and analogies customers use naturally

Step 3: CATEGORIZE
  - Before State (pains, problems, frustrations)
  - Dream State (desires, outcomes, aspirations)
  - Objections (reasons they hesitate)
  - Decision triggers (what pushed them to act)
  - Value language (how they describe post-purchase benefit)
  - Identity language (how they describe themselves)

Step 4: IDENTIFY PATTERNS
  - Which pain points recur most frequently?
  - Which desired outcomes appear repeatedly?
  - What objections show up across multiple sources?
  - What surprises you? (Unexpected patterns = most valuable)

Step 5: RANK by frequency and intensity
  - Pain mentioned by 40% outranks one mentioned by 5%
  - "This is ruining my business" outranks "it's kind of annoying"
  - Intersection of HIGH FREQUENCY + HIGH INTENSITY = primary message
```

### Copyhackers Spreadsheet Structure

| Column | Field | Example |
|--------|-------|---------|
| A | Source | Amazon 3-star review |
| B | Exact quote | "I was spending 3 hours a day just copying data between tools" |
| C | Category | Before State -- Pain |
| D | Subcategory | Time waste |
| E | Topic group | Manual data entry |
| F | Frequency | 14 similar quotes found |
| G | Usability | Headline potential, bullet point |

### AI-Native VOC Mining

An AI agent system performs VOC mining at scale that no human copywriter can
match. Using real-time research capabilities:

- Mine hundreds of reviews across multiple platforms in a single session
- Search Reddit threads, forum posts, and social comments simultaneously
- Extract and categorize phrases automatically into Before/Dream/Objection
- Identify frequency patterns across 500+ data points
- Generate the ranked message hierarchy directly from mining output
- Refresh VOC data regularly -- customer language evolves

### Common VOC Mining Mistakes

1. **Paraphrasing instead of quoting** -- You lose authenticity. Copy exact words.
2. **Small sample size** -- 10 quotes is anecdote. 100+ is data.
3. **Only mining positive reviews** -- 1-3 star reviews contain the richest data.
4. **Ignoring frequency** -- One unique phrase is a curiosity. Fifty similar
   phrases is a message.
5. **Mining once and stopping** -- Customer language evolves. Mine regularly.
6. **Skipping categorization** -- Uncategorized data is unusable data.
7. **Ignoring objections** -- Objections tell you what to address before the
   reader raises the concern.

## 3. Message Mining: From Raw VOC to Copy

Message mining is the practice of extracting exact phrases from VOC data that
can be dropped directly into copy as headlines, subheads, bullets, or CTAs.

### What Makes a Good Mined Message

- Vivid and specific, not generic
- Contains emotional weight
- Sounds like a real person talking
- Could work as a headline with minimal editing
- Multiple people say something similar (pattern, not outlier)

### Examples: Mined Messages Becoming Copy

```
AMAZON REVIEW:          "I finally stopped dreading Monday morning standups"
BECOMES HEADLINE:       Stop Dreading Monday Morning Standups

SUPPORT TICKET:         "I just need one place to see everything"
BECOMES SUBHEAD:        One Place to See Everything. Finally.

SURVEY RESPONSE:        "I was spending 3 hours a day just copying data"
BECOMES BULLET:         Eliminate the 3 hours/day you spend copying data
                        between tools

SALES CALL:             "What if it doesn't work for our weird workflow?"
BECOMES FAQ:            Works with your workflow -- even the weird parts

REDDIT THREAD:          "Every tool I've tried makes me feel dumber"
BECOMES PAIN SECTION:   You shouldn't need a PhD to manage your projects

G2 REVIEW:              "Set it up in lunch, had the team on it by 2pm"
BECOMES PROOF POINT:    Set up at lunch. Team onboarded by 2pm.
```

### The Message Hierarchy

After mining, build a message hierarchy -- a ranked list of messages from most
important to least:

```
LEVEL 1: PRIMARY MESSAGE (highest frequency + intensity)
  The single most compelling thing you can say
  This becomes your headline / hero section
  Example: "Stop losing 3 hours/day to manual data entry"

LEVEL 2: SUPPORTING MESSAGES (high frequency)
  3-5 messages that support and prove the primary message
  These become subheads, benefit sections, proof points
  Example: "One dashboard for everything"
  Example: "Set up in under 5 minutes"
  Example: "Works with the tools you already use"

LEVEL 3: OBJECTION HANDLERS (common objections from VOC)
  Messages that address what almost stopped people from buying
  These become FAQ, risk reversal, guarantee copy
  Example: "No credit card required to start"
  Example: "Works even if your team hates new tools"

LEVEL 4: PROOF AND SOCIAL PROOF
  Customer quotes, case study data, metrics
  These support claims made at Levels 1-3
  Example: "2,847 teams switched this quarter"
  Example: "'This saved my team 12 hours a week' -- Sarah K., Ops Lead"
```

## 4. The Rule of One

Joanna Wiebe's foundational principle for focused, high-converting copy:

### One Reader
- Write to one specific person, not a segment or demographic
- Picture them: their role, their frustrations, their vocabulary
- When you try to appeal to everyone, you convert no one

### One Big Idea
- Every page, email, or ad communicates one central idea
- If you cannot state it in a single sentence, you have too many ideas
- Everything on the page supports, proves, or advances this one idea

### One Promise
- Make one clear promise about the outcome
- Do not dilute with secondary promises
- The promise must be specific and believable

### One Offer
- Present one thing for the reader to do or buy
- One CTA path per page
- Multiple offers create decision paralysis and reduce conversions

### Rule of One Decision Matrix

| Element | Ask Yourself | If Multiple, Then... |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| Reader | Who is the ONE person this is for? | Create separate pages/emails |
| Idea | What is the ONE thing I am saying? | Cut until one remains |
| Promise | What is the ONE outcome they get? | Lead with strongest, support rest |
| Offer | What is the ONE action to take? | Remove competing CTAs |

## 6. PAS Framework (Problem-Agitate-Solve)

Dan Kennedy called PAS the most reliable sales copywriting formula ever invented.
Copyhackers treats agitation as the most critical element -- the part most
copywriters underwrite.

### Problem
- Open with the problem the prospect actually feels
- Use their language (from VOC mining), not yours
- Make it specific, pressing, and relatable
- Name the exact frustration, not a generic category problem

**Example:**
"You open your laptop Monday morning and there are 47 unread Slack messages, 12
emails marked urgent, and three tools that all show different numbers for last
week's revenue."

### Agitate
- This is where most copy fails: writers rush to the solution
- Intensify the discomfort by elaborating consequences
- Show what happens if the problem continues unsolved
- Stir emotions: frustration, fear, embarrassment, wasted money, lost time
- Use sensory and emotional language
- The agitation should make the reader nod and think "yes, exactly"

**Example:**
"So you spend the next two hours reconciling data instead of doing actual work.
By the time you have a number you trust, it's lunchtime. Your boss has already
asked twice. Your team thinks you're slow. And tomorrow? Same thing."

### Solve
- Present your product as the resolution
- Connect the solution directly to the agitated pain
- Be specific about what changes for them
- Include proof (testimonials, data, demos)

**Example:**
"Acme Dashboard pulls from every tool automatically. One number. Updated live.
You open your laptop and the answer is already there. Takes 4 minutes to set up."

### Extended Variant: PASOP (for Email Sequences)

```
Email 1: Problem > Agitate > Solve > Outcome > (new) Problem
Email 2: Pick up new problem > Agitate > Solve > Outcome > (new) Problem
Email 3: Pick up new problem > Agitate > Solve > link to offer

This creates narrative continuity and open loops across a drip sequence.
Each email ends by introducing a new problem the reader recognizes,
compelling them to open the next email.
```

## 7. AIDA Framework (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action)

Over a century old, adapted by Copyhackers for SaaS and digital products:

### Attention
- Lead with the OUTCOME the prospect wants, not your product name
- Use a headline that calls out their specific situation or role
- Pattern-interrupt: say something unexpected or counterintuitive
- Example: "Your onboarding emails are losing you 40% of trial users"

### Interest
- Introduce the mechanism that makes your solution different
- Share a surprising data point, story, or VOC-derived insight
- Build curiosity about how the solution works
- Show the "before and after" of using the product

### Desire
- Stack benefits, not features
- Use social proof: customer quotes, logos, case study snippets
- Paint the "dream state" using customer language
- Address objections before the reader raises them
- Show the product in action (screenshots, GIFs, demos)

### Action
- Single, clear CTA aligned with awareness level
- Remove friction: minimize form fields, clarify what happens next
- Call-to-value for prospects still deciding ("Show me how")
- Call-to-action for prospects ready to buy ("Start my free trial")

## 8. The 1-2-3-4 Formula

A four-part structure ensuring copy answers every prospect's core questions:

1. **What I've got** -- Clear description. No jargon. Just clarity.
2. **What it'll do for you** -- Primary benefit. Answer "so what?" immediately.
3. **Who am I?** -- Credibility. Credentials, track record, social proof.
4. **What you need to do next** -- One specific action. Clearly stated.

Every step answers a question every prospect has before converting. If any piece
is missing, the argument has a hole.

## 9. Headline Formulas (15 Concrete Formulas With Examples)

### Formula 1: [Pain Point] + [Desired Outcome]
"Stop guessing which campaigns drive revenue. See it in real time."

### Formula 2: How to [Achieve Outcome] Without [Pain/Objection]
"How to onboard new hires in 48 hours without the 6-week training program"

### Formula 3: [Number] Ways to [Achieve Outcome]
"7 ways to cut your SaaS churn rate this quarter"

### Formula 4: [Specific Result] in [Timeframe]
"Double your demo bookings in 90 days"

### Formula 5: The [Audience Role] Who [Achieved Result]
"The ops manager who cut reporting time from 12 hours to 12 minutes"

### Formula 6: Why [Common Belief] Is Wrong (And What to Do Instead)
"Why 'more content' is killing your conversion rate (and what to do instead)"

### Formula 7: [Outcome] for [Specific Audience]
"Enterprise-grade security for teams that hate enterprise software"

### Formula 8: What [X Number] of [Audience] Know About [Topic]
"What 2,400 SaaS founders know about reducing churn that you don't"

### Formula 9: [Do This], Not [That]
"Write emails people reply to, not emails people delete"

### Formula 10: Finally, [Dream State]
"Finally, a CRM your sales team will actually use"

### Formula 11: [VOC Quote as Headline]
"'I stopped dreading Monday morning standups'"

### Formula 12: [Outcome] -- Without [Sacrifice]
"Ship 2x faster -- without burning out your engineering team"

### Formula 13: The [Adjective] Way to [Outcome]
"The 4-minute way to reconcile all your revenue data"

### Formula 14: Are You Still [Pain Point]?
"Are you still manually copying data between three different dashboards?"

### Formula 15: [Product] Helps [Audience] [Outcome] So They Can [Bigger Outcome]
"Acme Dashboard helps ops teams see all their data in one place so they can
make decisions in minutes, not days"

### Headline Writing Process (Copyhackers + Ogilvy Merged)

1. Start with VOC data -- pull exact phrases that could be headlines
2. Apply 5+ formulas to the top messages from your hierarchy
3. Write 20+ headline variations minimum (Ogilvy wrote 26 for Rolls-Royce)
4. Run every headline through the "So What?" test
5. Check: does it promise a clear, specific benefit?
6. Check: does it speak to the One Reader?
7. Check: is it clear without context? (Ogilvy: standalone selling proposition)
8. Select top 5 for A/B testing recommendation
9. Ensure at least 2 use exact VOC language
10. Include both pain-focused and outcome-focused options

## 10. The "So What?" Test

Apply to every single line of copy:

```
Step 1:  Read the line of copy
Step 2:  Ask "So what? Why should the reader care?"
Step 3:  If no answer --> rewrite with a benefit or outcome
Step 4:  Then ask "Prove it!" --> back the claim with evidence
```

### Before / After Examples

| Before (fails "So What?") | After (passes) |
|---------------------------|----------------|
| "Our platform uses AI-powered analytics" | "See exactly which campaigns drive revenue -- in real time" |
| "We have 10 years of experience" | "We've optimized 2,400+ campaigns across 18 industries" |
| "Industry-leading performance" | "Processes 2.4M transactions/second -- 3x faster than the nearest competitor" |
| "Seamless integration" | "Connects to Salesforce, HubSpot, and Slack in under 2 minutes" |
| "Best-in-class support" | "Average response time: 4 minutes. 98.7% satisfaction rating." |
| "Trusted by thousands" | "Used by 2,847 teams including Shopify, Stripe, and Notion" |

### The Rule
The bigger the claim, the more proof you need. A small claim needs a data point.
A big claim needs testimonials, case studies, demos, and guarantees.

## 11. Specificity: Replace Vague With Proof

Vague copy is dead copy. Specificity builds believability.

### The Specificity Sweep

Review every line and ask: "Am I being vague? Can I be more specific?"

| Vague | Specific |
|-------|----------|
| "many customers" | "2,847 teams" |
| "fast setup" | "set up in 4 minutes" |
| "affordable" | "$29/mo -- less than your daily coffee" |
| "top-rated" | "4.8 stars on G2 from 1,200 reviews" |
| "improves results" | "increases demo bookings by 34%" |
| "powerful features" | "automates 47 manual steps per report" |
| "saves time" | "saves 12 hours per team per week" |
| "enterprise-grade" | "SOC 2 Type II certified, 99.99% uptime" |

### Why Specificity Works
Specific claims activate the reader's analytical mind positively. Vague claims
trigger skepticism. "Increase conversions" sounds like marketing. "Increase
trial-to-paid conversions by 22% in 90 days" sounds like proof.

## 12. Button Copy and CTA Principles

Buttons are the place of conversion. You cannot convert online without clicking
at least one CTA.

### Core Principles

1. **First-person language** -- "Start my free trial" outperforms "Start your
   free trial." First-person creates ownership.

2. **Action-oriented verbs** -- Lead with what the user gets or does.
   "Get my report" not "Submit." "Show me the plan" not "Continue."

3. **Specificity** -- Name what happens after the click.
   "Download the 2024 Benchmark Report" not "Download now."

4. **Call to Value vs. Call to Action**
   - Call to Value: for prospects still deciding. "Show me how to double
     my conversions"
   - Call to Action: for prospects who have decided. "Start my free trial"

5. **Reduce anxiety near the button** -- Add microcopy:
   "No credit card required" / "Cancel anytime" / "Takes 30 seconds"

6. **One primary CTA per section** -- No competing buttons at equal weight.

### CTA Scoring Matrix

| Criterion | Score 0 | Score 1 | Score 2 |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|
| First person? | "Your" or absent | Mixed | "My" / "Me" / "I" |
| Action verb? | No verb / "Submit" | Generic verb | Specific action |
| Specific outcome? | No outcome stated | Implied outcome | Explicit outcome |
| Anxiety reducer? | None | Generic ("Safe") | Specific ("No CC needed") |
| Matches awareness? | Wrong level | Neutral | Matched perfectly |

## 13. Email Copy Structure

### Subject Lines (Copyhackers Principles)

- **Curiosity gaps**: hint at value without revealing it
- **Empty suitcase**: use "this" without a noun ("This changed everything")
- **Endorsement**: reference an authority or customer quote
- **Specificity**: numbers, names, details increase opens
- **Open loops**: tease content that resolves only by opening
- Keep under 50 characters for mobile
- A/B test subject lines rigorously

### Preview Text
- Complements the subject line, does not repeat it
- Does not give away the curiosity
- Second reason to open
- Treat as subtitle, not afterthought

### Body Structure
- **Hook**: first 1-2 sentences earn the next sentence
- **One idea per email**: do not cover multiple topics
- **Conversational tone**: smart friend, not brand
- **Short paragraphs**: 1-3 sentences max
- **Single CTA**: one link/button, repeated if email is long
- **P.S. line**: high-readership zone -- use for urgency or secondary hook

### Email Subject Line Examples

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Curiosity gap | "The metric your dashboard is hiding" |
| Empty suitcase | "This is why your churn rate won't drop" |
| Specificity | "3 subject line tweaks that lifted opens 41%" |
| Pain callout | "Still reconciling data manually?" |
| Social proof | "How Sarah's team cut reporting by 12 hrs/week" |
| Outcome | "One dashboard. Zero guesswork." |

### Copy Length Decision Framework

Based on Eugene Schwartz's five awareness levels:

| Awareness Level | Copy Length | Rationale |
|----------------|------------|-----------|
| Most Aware | Short | They know you. Just give the deal. |
| Product Aware | Short-Medium | They know what you sell. Reassure them. |
| Solution Aware | Medium-Long | They want the result, don't know you yet. |
| Problem Aware | Long | They feel the problem, need education. |
| Completely Unaware | Very Long | Must move through all stages. |

**Governing principle:** Your page needs to be as long as necessary to make the
argument that addresses the prospect in their current state of awareness. No
longer, no shorter.

## 15. The Seven Sweeps (Copy Editing Framework)

Joanna Wiebe's 7-step framework for fast, confident copy editing:

```
SWEEP 1: CLARITY
  Is every sentence immediately understandable?
  Remove jargon. Simplify structure. Reader never re-reads a line.

SWEEP 2: VOICE AND TONE
  Does this sound like the brand?
  Check vocabulary, cadence, personality. Match audience.

SWEEP 3: "SO WHAT?"
  Does every line answer why the reader should care?
  Cut or rewrite anything that fails.

SWEEP 4: "PROVE IT"
  Is every claim backed by evidence?
  Add testimonials, data, demos, screenshots, case studies.

SWEEP 5: SPECIFICITY
  Replace vague with precise.
  Numbers, names, timeframes, outcomes.

SWEEP 6: HEIGHTENED EMOTION
  Does the copy make the reader FEEL something?
  Inject sensory language, tension, or relief.
  Watch for friction words that make the task sound tedious.

SWEEP 7: ZERO RISK
  Have you eliminated all perceived risk?
  Guarantees, reduced commitment, anxiety reduction,
  clarity on what happens after the click.
```

**Start and finish with Clarity.** It is the most important sweep and makes
every subsequent sweep more effective.

## 16. A/B Testing Copy

### What to Test (Priority Order)

1. **Headlines** -- highest impact, test first
2. **CTA button copy** -- second highest impact
3. **Page opening** (first 100 words) -- affects scroll depth
4. **Social proof placement** -- can dramatically shift trust
5. **Copy length** -- short vs. long based on awareness hypothesis
6. **Pain-focused vs. outcome-focused** -- which emotional direction converts
7. **VOC-derived vs. brand-voice** -- which resonates with this audience

### Testing Principles

- Test ONE element at a time for clean results
- Always test VOC-derived copy against brand-voice copy
- Run tests to statistical significance (not gut feel)
- Document results and update your VOC swipe file with performance data
- Winning VOC phrases become permanent copy; losers get replaced

### Expected Outcomes

- VOC-derived copy typically outperforms brand-voice for cold audiences
- Brand-voice may perform better for highly aware, loyal audiences
- The most effective copy often blends VOC language with brand tone
- Specificity (from VOC) almost always beats vagueness (from assumptions)

## 17. Failure Modes: Copy That Does Not Convert

### Failure Mode 1: Benefit-less Features
**Symptom:** Copy lists what the product does without explaining why it matters.
**Example:** "Real-time sync across all devices"
**Fix:** "Never miss an update -- every change shows up on every device instantly"

### Failure Mode 2: "We" Language
**Symptom:** Copy talks about the company instead of the reader.
**Example:** "We are proud to announce our new AI-powered platform"
**Fix:** "You now have AI doing the analysis that used to take your team 6 hours"

### Failure Mode 3: Jargon and Buzzwords
**Symptom:** Copy uses industry terms the reader does not recognize.
**Example:** "Leverage our omnichannel synergies for optimal KPI alignment"
**Fix:** "Reach your customers wherever they are -- email, social, or in-app"

### Failure Mode 4: Vague Superlatives
**Symptom:** Copy uses adjectives without evidence.
**Example:** "The best, most powerful, industry-leading solution"
**Fix:** "Rated #1 on G2 for 3 consecutive quarters (1,847 reviews)"

### Failure Mode 5: Missing or Weak CTA
**Symptom:** No clear next step, or CTA says "Submit" / "Learn more"
**Fix:** "Start my free trial -- no credit card required"

### Failure Mode 6: Skipping Agitation
**Symptom:** Copy jumps from problem to solution too quickly.
**Fix:** Spend 2-3x more copy on the agitation than you think necessary.

### Failure Mode 7: Clever Over Clear
**Symptom:** Headline is a pun or wordplay that requires context to understand.
**Example:** "Time flies when you're having funds!" (financial app)
**Fix:** "See where every dollar goes -- in real time"

### Failure Mode 8: No Research
**Symptom:** Copy sounds like it was written from brand assumptions, not data.
**Fix:** Mine VOC before writing a single word. If research is not the biggest
part of the work, you are doing it wrong.

## 18. AI-Native Conversion Copywriting

### How AI Agents Apply This Methodology

An AI agent applies Copyhackers methodology with unique advantages:

**Scale:** Mine 500+ reviews in a session vs. a human's 50.
**Speed:** Generate 20+ headline variations using all 15 formulas simultaneously.
**Consistency:** Apply the Seven Sweeps systematically every time. Never skip a
sweep due to deadline pressure.
**Variation:** Produce copy for 5 awareness levels in a single output.
**Testing:** Generate matched A/B test pairs with documented hypotheses.

### What the AI Agent Must Never Do

- Write copy without research. VOC mining comes first. Always.
- Generate copy that sounds "AI-ish" -- generic, hedging, disclaimer-heavy
- Produce a single copy version when multiple should be tested
- Skip the "So What?" and "Prove It" tests on any line
- Use the word "leverage," "synergy," "cutting-edge," or "game-changer"
- Write benefits without evidence
- Forget the Rule of One

| Principle | Summary |
|-----------|---------|
| Data over opinion | Write from VOC research, not assumptions |
| Clarity first | If they cannot understand it, nothing else matters |

## 19. AI Copy: Trust Framework (2025+)

Source: Copyhackers, "AI's Trust Problem" (December 2025).

### When AI Copy Helps vs. Hurts Trust

```
AI COPY HELPS when:                    AI COPY HURTS when:
- Generating volume variations         - It IS the final customer-facing message
- Brainstorming angles/hooks           - Trust is the buying bottleneck (YMYL)
- Adapting copy across platforms        - Customers can detect it (55% detection rate)
- Drafting first versions for editing   - B2B where trust scores are already low (61/100)
- VOC data synthesis and pattern ID     - Replacing customer-sourced language with generic
```

### Decision Tree: Should AI Write This Copy?

```
Is this YMYL content (health, finance, legal)?
  YES --> Human writes/heavily edits. AI drafts only. Medical/legal review required.
  NO  |
      v
Is this the primary sales message (headline, hero, CTA)?
  YES --> AI generates options, human selects and refines using VOC data.
  NO  |
      v
Is this high-volume adaptation (social variants, email subject lines)?
  YES --> AI handles generation with brand voice guardrails. Human spot-checks.
  NO  --> Evaluate case by case. Default to human for trust-sensitive contexts.
```

### AI Copy Guardrails

| Guardrail | Rule |
|-----------|------|
| VOC primacy | AI must work FROM customer language, not replace it |
| Detection risk | Run "AI detection" sweep on customer-facing copy; rewrite flagged sections |
| Brand voice lock | Define voice attributes BEFORE AI generates; reject off-brand output |
| Claim verification | Every specific claim in AI-generated copy must be fact-checked |
| Trust-sensitive override | For YMYL domains, health claims, financial promises: human-only final copy |
| Transparency | If customers will know AI was involved, acknowledge it (builds trust vs. deception) |
